# Framework: How SEO, AEO, and GEO Actually Differ

The assumption most content teams make: if your SEO is solid, everything else follows.

It's a reasonable assumption. It's also wrong.

Search has fractured into three distinct surfaces, each governed by different systems, rewarding different content behaviors, and failing in different ways. A piece of content can rank on page one of Google, never appear in a featured snippet, and remain completely invisible to AI systems — all at the same time.

Understanding why requires looking at each surface on its own terms.

---

## SEO: Optimizing for search engines

Search Engine Optimization is the oldest of the three disciplines, and the most familiar. The goal is ranking — getting your content to appear near the top of results when someone searches a relevant query.

Search engines like Google and Bing evaluate content through hundreds of signals. The ones that matter most in 2026:

- **Authority** — Do credible sites link to yours?
- **Relevance** — Does your content match what the searcher actually wants?
- **Technical health** — Does your page load fast, work on mobile, and follow indexing best practices?
- **Experience signals** — Do people who land on your page stay, or do they leave immediately?

SEO rewards consistency over time. A page doesn't rank because it's well-written. It ranks because it's well-written, technically sound, and trusted by other sites on the web. That trust accumulates slowly.

The writing behavior SEO rewards: comprehensive coverage of a topic, clear structure with descriptive headings, and content that matches the intent behind a search query — not just the keywords.

---

## AEO: Optimizing for answer engines

Answer Engine Optimization targets a different outcome entirely. The goal isn't to rank a page — it's to have your content extracted and displayed as a direct answer, without the user necessarily clicking through to your site.

Answer engines include:

- **Featured snippets** — The boxed answer at the top of Google results
- **People Also Ask** — The expandable question clusters in search results
- **Voice search** — Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant reading a single answer aloud
- **Knowledge panels** — The structured information boxes that appear for entities (people, organizations, places)

The critical distinction: SEO gets you on the list. AEO gets you read aloud.

These are different jobs. A page optimized for ranking may be too long, too nuanced, or too conversational to be extracted as a clean answer. Answer engines need content that is structured, specific, and formatted in ways that make extraction easy.

The writing behavior AEO rewards: direct answers in the first sentence, question-formatted headings (H2s and H3s phrased as actual questions), concise definitions in the 40–60 word range, and structured lists that map cleanly to discrete steps or categories.

---

## GEO: Optimizing for generative engines

Generative Engine Optimization is the newest discipline — and the least understood. The goal is being cited or synthesized by AI systems when they generate responses to user queries.

Generative engines include:

- **ChatGPT** (with Browse or search enabled)
- **Perplexity**
- **Google AI Overviews**
- **Microsoft Copilot**

These systems don't rank pages. They read broadly, synthesize across sources, and decide what to include in a generated response. That decision is governed by different signals than traditional search.

Princeton research identified the factors that most reliably improve visibility in AI-generated responses:

- **Citing sources** — Content that references named authorities gets included more often
- **Including statistics** — Specific numbers with units and dates signal credibility
- **Adding expert quotes** — Attribution to recognized authorities increases citation likelihood
- **Entity clarity** — Proper nouns, branded terms, and specific names help AI systems understand what your content is about
- **Freshness signals** — Dates, "as of 2026," and recent examples signal currency

The research found that applying these signals produced visibility boosts of 30–40% in AI-generated responses.

The writing behavior GEO rewards: authoritative sourcing, specific data, named experts, and content structured so that a key claim can be lifted and cited without losing its meaning.

---

## Why the three require different strategies

Here's where most content guides go wrong. They treat SEO, AEO, and GEO as variations of the same optimization problem — as if better content naturally performs across all three surfaces.

It doesn't. And here's why:

| | SEO | AEO | GEO |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Target system** | Search algorithm | Extraction engine | Language model |
| **Success metric** | Page ranking | Answer selection | Citation / synthesis |
| **Content length** | Comprehensive | Concise | Credible |
| **Key signal** | Authority + relevance | Structure + directness | Sources + specificity |
| **Time to results** | Months | Weeks | Variable |

A comprehensive, long-form piece may rank well on Google while being too dense for AEO extraction. A tightly formatted FAQ may perform well in featured snippets while lacking the sourcing and entity density that GEO requires. A heavily cited, data-rich piece may be ideal for AI citation while being too formal for the conversational queries that drive voice search.

These aren't contradictions you have to solve by writing three versions of everything. They're tensions you resolve by understanding which surface matters most for a given piece of content — then layering in signals for the others intentionally.

---

## The layering principle

Think of the three disciplines as layers, not competing approaches.

**Foundation layer (SEO)** — Every piece of content needs this. Technical health, clear structure, topic coverage, and intent matching. Without this foundation, nothing else works.

**Extraction layer (AEO)** — Add this when you want content surfaced as a direct answer. Question-formatted headings, concise definitions, structured lists. Apply selectively based on content type.

**Citation layer (GEO)** — Add this when you want AI systems to include your content in generated responses. Named sources, specific statistics, expert attribution, entity-rich language. Apply when credibility and reach matter more than click-through.

Most content benefits from a strong foundation layer plus one or two elements from the extraction and citation layers. The modules in this playbook show you how to apply each layer in practice.

---

## A note on how fast this is changing

AI search behavior is not stable. The signals that drive GEO visibility today may shift as models are updated, as platforms change their sourcing behavior, and as the volume of AI-optimized content grows.

SEO has been around long enough to have relatively stable principles. AEO has matured considerably since featured snippets launched. GEO is still early.

That's not a reason to ignore it. It's a reason to treat your GEO approach as a hypothesis you test and update — not a checklist you complete once and file away.
